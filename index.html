Did you know?

Humans can typically count up to 7 items in a single glance without losing track.

This phenomenon is known as "subitizing," and it highlights the limitations of our working memory and attention span.

Beyond this number, we often need to group items or use counting strategies to keep track accurately.

In cognitive psychology, the "Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two" is a theory proposed by George A. Miller in 1956.

It suggests that the average number of objects an individual can hold in their working memory is about seven, with a range of five to nine.

This concept has been influential in understanding human cognition and memory capacity.

One of the world’s most famous counting animals was Alex, an African Grey Parrot studied by Irene Pepperberg.

It could count sets of objects up to 6 and was in the process of learning 7 and 8 before he passed away in 2007.

Alex was the first animal to spontaneously use a label for "zero" (he used the word "none" to describe the absence of quantity), a concept that takes human children years to master.

Ai 🦍(Japanese: アイ), (died Jan 09, 2026 of old age, RIP) a chimpanzee studied by Tetsuro Matsuzawa, was able to count sets of objects, and order numbers in sequences.

Matsuzawa reported that the chimpanzee Ai was able to report the number of objects presented to it by selecting the corresponding key from six keys marked with the Arabic numerals 1-to-6, suggesting that Ai had the ability to use the symbols of abstract numbers, or, in other words, that Ai had the ability to count.

Other studies in the Ai project also find that upon training, some chimpanzee (Ai's son Ayumu) outperformed human (some university students) in numerical tasks, showing that chimpanzees have a remarkable working memory
